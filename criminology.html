What criminology actually is

Criminology is the study of crime — its constitution through law, its character and causes, the contexts and consequences of its commission, and the social responses it provokes.

It is not criminal justice. It is not true crime entertainment. It is not a synonym for "criminal psychology." Criminology is a discipline with a 150-year history, a defined methodology, and a stubborn commitment to finding out what is true rather than what feels right.

What that means

Constitution through law. Crime is not a natural fact. An act becomes a crime because the law defines it as one. What counts as a crime is made, not given, and it changes across jurisdictions and eras. This is also why only some harmful acts are criminalized, and only some people who commit them are treated as criminals.

Character and causes. What the act is, and why it happens. Rarely a single cause. Usually a set of conditions acting together.

Contexts of commission. The conditions under which crime is actually committed. The same act can carry a different meaning in a different setting.

Consequences of commission. The impacts and harms that follow — for victims, for communities, and for institutions.

The social responses it provokes. Policing, law, policy, punishment, prevention. How a society answers crime, and whether the answer fits the problem.

How criminologists know what they know

Criminology is an empirical discipline. That means it relies on evidence that can be observed, measured, and tested. The methods include:

  • Large-scale statistical analysis — of police records, victimization surveys, court outcomes, and administrative data
  • Qualitative fieldwork — interviews, ethnography, and observation in neighborhoods, courts, and prisons
  • Experiments and quasi-experiments — testing whether a specific intervention actually changes outcomes
  • Historical and comparative analysis — examining how crime and control vary across societies and eras
  • Systematic review and meta-analysis — synthesizing findings across hundreds of studies to identify robust patterns

The discipline is deliberately eclectic. Crime is too complex for any single method to capture it. The trade-off is that criminologists must be methodologically literate across several traditions, which is hard. The reward is that genuinely good criminology is hard to dismiss.

What criminology is not

These distinctions matter because confusion between them leads to bad policy and wasted money.

Criminology is not criminal justice

Criminal justice is the study of institutions — police, courts, corrections. Criminology is broader. It studies why crime happens in the first place, not merely how the state responds. A criminal justice scholar might study whether longer sentences reduce reoffending. A criminologist might ask whether the definition of "reoffending" itself is flawed, or whether the sentence causes the very behavior it claims to deter.

Criminology is not true crime entertainment

True crime is a storytelling genre. It selects for narrative satisfaction — clear villains, surprising twists, emotional resolution. Criminology selects for truth, which is often messy, uncertain, and narratively unsatisfying. True crime makes people feel they understand crime. Criminology often shows them they do not. These are opposing goals.

Criminology is not a political position

Criminologists hold political views like everyone else. But the discipline's claim to authority rests on method, not ideology. When a criminologist says "deterrence does not work for this category of offense," that should be based on evidence, not on a prior commitment to abolition or punishment. The public is right to be skeptical of experts who always find evidence for their pre-existing views.

Why it matters

Bad criminology is expensive. Not just in money — though the costs of incarceration, failed rehabilitation programs, and reactive policing run into billions — but in human lives. People sentenced under theories of deterrence that do not deter. Communities surveilled under theories of prevention that do not prevent. Victims ignored because their crimes do not fit the dominant narrative.

Good criminology is rare, but it has produced genuine knowledge. We know that crime is heavily concentrated in a small number of places and among a small number of people. We know that most people age out of crime. We know that legitimacy — the belief that authorities are fair — predicts compliance better than severity of punishment. We know that early intervention can work, but only when well-designed and sustained.

These findings do not map neatly onto political positions. They are inconvenient for almost everyone. That is a feature, not a bug. It is what makes criminology worth doing.

Criminology is not the study of criminals. It is the study of the processes by which some acts become defined as crimes, some people become defined as criminals, and some responses become defined as justice.
