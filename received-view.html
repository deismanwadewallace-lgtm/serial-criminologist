The received view

The collection of assumptions about crime that most people carry around without realizing where they came from — and without checking whether they are true.

Crime is the most over-reported and most misunderstood subject in the news. Gregg Barak called the criminologist's response to this "newsmaking criminology" — the deliberate work of contesting and demystifying how crime is represented.1 Correcting the received view is not point-scoring. It is part of returning honest knowledge to a public that has been served distortion.

Where it comes from

The received view is not a conspiracy. It is the sediment of decades of media coverage, political messaging, entertainment, and ordinary conversation. It forms in childhood, hardens in adolescence, and rarely gets examined again. By the time most people think about crime as a policy question, they already "know" what crime is, who commits it, and what stops it. The problem is that much of this knowledge is wrong.

The received view has three main sources:

  1. Media coverage — Crime news selects for drama, not frequency. Violent crime is overrepresented; mundane crime is invisible. The result is a systematically distorted picture of what crime looks like and who is affected by it.
  2. Political rhetoric — Politicians need simple stories with clear villains. "Tough on crime" messaging has been effective electorally for decades, regardless of its empirical basis. The same is true for simplistic "root causes" narratives on the left.
  3. Entertainment — Television, film, and now podcasts create powerful narratives about crime that feel true because they are coherent. Real crime is rarely coherent. Real criminals are rarely masterminds or monsters. Real victims are rarely pure. Entertainment must simplify to work; simplification is distortion.

The core assumptions

These are the specific beliefs that make up the received view. None of them is entirely false. All of them are more complicated than they appear.

"Crime is rising"

In most developed countries, violent crime has been falling for thirty years. Property crime has also declined. This is one of the most robust findings in criminology, and one of the least known by the public. The perception of rising crime often tracks media coverage or specific high-profile incidents, not actual trends.

"Criminals are different from the rest of us"

There are persistent offenders, and there are people who commit terrible acts. But the vast majority of people who commit crimes are not fundamentally different from people who do not. They age out. They respond to circumstances. They are often more impulsive, more exposed to violence, and more disadvantaged — but these are differences of degree, not kind. The "criminal type" is a 19th-century myth that persists because it is comforting.

"Tougher sentences deter crime"

The deterrence effect of sentence severity is weak to nonexistent for most offenses. People do not commit crimes because they have done a rational cost-benefit analysis. They commit crimes because of impulse, opportunity, pressure, intoxication, or because they do not expect to be caught at all. What does deter is the certainty of being caught, not the severity of the punishment.

"The police solve most crimes"

Clearance rates for most crimes are low. For property crimes, often under 20%. For sexual offenses, historically under 10%. The image of detectives diligently solving cases is largely a fiction created by media. Most crimes go unsolved, and most victims never see justice in the formal sense.

"Victims are random and blameless"

Some victims are indeed random targets of stranger violence. But victimization is not randomly distributed. It clusters by place, by social network, by lifestyle, and by relationship. Many victims know their offenders. Many are themselves involved in risky behavior. This does not make them responsible for their victimization, but it does mean that the "innocent victim" narrative, while politically useful, is statistically incomplete.

What the received view costs

Believing false things about crime has real consequences. It leads to policies that do not work, resources that are wasted, and people who are hurt.

  • Mass incarceration built on the assumption that prison deters — evidence shows weak deterrence and significant criminogenic effects
  • Mandatory minimums that remove judicial discretion and produce manifestly unjust outcomes
  • Policing strategies that target visible disorder while ignoring serious harm that does not look like crime
  • Victim services that are designed around a stereotyped victim who does not match most actual victims
  • Public fear that is disconnected from actual risk, distorting how people live and how resources are allocated

The first step toward better crime policy is not more data. It is admitting that much of what we believe is folklore dressed up as common sense.
